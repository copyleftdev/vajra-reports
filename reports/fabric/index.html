Fabric is an AI prompt augmentation framework created by Daniel Miessler that has captured 40.5K stars in under two years. But the project behind those stars tells a story the star count cannot: a silent power transition. The creator has stepped back from active development, and a single volunteer maintainer — Kayvan Sylvan (ksylvan) — now writes 84.9% of all human commits and merges 98% of his own PRs. This is the most extreme single-maintainer dependency we have measured across any project in our portfolio.

Key Finding: The Invisible Hand danielmiessler created Fabric and owns the repo's 40.5K stars, but has only 6 commits in the last 800 (1.0%). ksylvan writes 84.9% of all human code, reviews all community PRs, and responds to 843 issue comments. Fabric is a one-person project wearing a two-person mask. If ksylvan steps away, active development stops.

The Succession Pattern: Creator to Delegate

The all-time git shortlog tells the full story. Daniel Miessler built Fabric from scratch (773 all-time commits), but starting mid-2025, Kayvan Sylvan took over active development (949 all-time commits, and 510 of the last 600 human commits). This is a delegation succession: the creator retains ownership and brand authority while a delegate does all the engineering work.

The BDFL Paradox: Present in Name, Absent in Code danielmiessler has 0 PRs in our 500-PR sample and only 6 commits (mostly README/docs) in the last 800 commits. His most recent code contribution was January 2026 (a docs update). Yet the project carries his name, his 40.5K stars, and his brand. ksylvan is the de facto BDFL doing the work; danielmiessler is the de jure owner holding the keys.

The Delegation Model vs the BDFL Model Fabric is not a classic BDFL project (where the creator codes everything). It is a delegation model: the creator retains ownership and sets direction while a delegate handles all engineering. The risk is that the delegate has all the institutional knowledge but none of the ownership leverage.
